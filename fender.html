Problem Summary

This project was something I worked on at my time at Tigercat. As a product design intern, I was responsible for the continuous improvement of the 1165 harvester. As the system is in low volume production, our team would monitor all active systems, and be tasked with solving any issues that were reported from the field.

This case in particular was an issue where the fender was impacted and bent, interfering with the feed roller teeth.

Solution

My Work

My process was systematic and simple; I first analyzed the images received and determined the root cause of the deflection. It appeared that the large weight-reduction cut outs were causing the fender to locally deform.

The most likely impact area is near the outer edge of the fender. This problem can be simplified as a cantilever beam. The force applied to the end of the beam will result in a certain amount of deflection, which can be reduced according to the deflection equation deflection = Pl^3/3EI

a) Reducing the load applied (P)
b) Changing cross sectional shape (I)
c) Reducing length (L)
d) Changing material (E)
e) Increase the beam depth
f) Adding structural reinforcements

A and C are out of the question. I cannot simply reduce the load applied or reduce the length, as it needed to cover the full width of the rollers. B, D, E, and F were all factors I considered.

Without going through too many details, the end result was that I increased the thickness of the plate slightly, and reduced the size of the cutouts. I considered several different designs when coming to this conclusion, running a standard FEA static study to compare results. Based on the theory, increasing the thickness would increased the "beam's depth", which increased the area moment of inertia as shown in the bottom left image.
